Zeus and his lovers

Latona: mother of Apollo and Diana; turned the rustics into frogs.

Io: turned into a heifer watched by Argus; Mercury killed Argus; Hera sent a gadfly to torment Io; Jupiter turned her back.

Semele: mother of Bacchus.

Callisto: turned into a bear by Juno, and turned into the Great and Little Bear by Jupiter.

Danae: locked in a bronze chamber and Zeus came to her in the form of golden rain and Perseus was born.

Alcmene: gave birth to Heracles.

Europa: carried away by Zeus in the disguise of a bull.

Apollo and Artemis

Syrinx and Pan

Silenus: schoolmaster and foster-father of Bacchus and was taken to Midas when he was drunk.

Midas: whatever he touch should be changed into gold, recovered after he washed his guilt in the River Pactolus. He became a worshipper of Pan (the god of the fields) . Once Pan wanted to compare his music with Apollo. All the audiences thought Apollo won but Midas. So Apollo turned Midas' ears into ass' ears. Only his hair-dresser knew this secret.

Hyacinthus and Apollo: Apollo was very fond of a youth named Hyacinthus. One day they played the game of throwing discus, the discus thrown by Apollo was bounded by the earth and struck Hyacinthus' forehead. His blood dropped on the herbage and the herbage became flowers. It was also said that Zephyrus (the West wind) who was jealous of Apollo changed the trajectory of the quoit.

Clytie: a water-nymph and in love with Apollo, she gazed the sun nine days without eating or drinking and became the sunflower.

Marsyas: Minerva invented the flute but was laughed by Cupid when playing. She threw the flute down to the earth and it was picked up by Marsyas. He blew it and wanted to challenge Apollo. Apollo won and punished him by flaying him alive.

Endymion: a beautiful youth who fed his flock on Mount Latmos. One calm, clear night, Diana looked down and saw him sleeping. The goddess was conquered by his beauty and went down to watch over his sleeping. Endymion became the symbol of young poet. His fancy and his heart seeking in vain for that which can satisfy them, finding his favorite hour in the quiet moonlight,

Orion: the son of Neptune, a handsome giant and a mighty hunter. He loved Merope but her father constantly deferred his consent. Orion attempted to gain her by violence but his behavior made her father angry. Her father made Orion drunk and blinded him. Orion came to Vulcan's forge. Vulcan sent Kedalion as his guide, and Orion went to the sun-god and restored his sight. After he dwelt as a hunter with Diana and was about to marry her. Apollo was unhappy and tricked Diana to shoot Orion's head. Diana placed him among the stars with a girdle, sword and Sirius, his dog, and the Pleiads.

Actaeon and Diana: Actaeon was the son of King Cadmus. Once he hunted the stag in the mountains with his companions, when they were resting Actaeon strayed into Diana's cave and saw her washing. Diana turned him into a stag and Actaeon was killed by his own followers.

Hippolytus: Theseus' son and was loved by Theseus' wife Phaedra. Theseus imprecated the vengeance of Neptune upon him. Diana called Aesculapius to revive him and took him to Italy.

Aesculapius: Apollo's son and had the power to restore the dead to life. Pluto was shocked and called Jupiter to launch a thunderbolt on him. Apollo was angry after his' son's dead and he punished the workers of Vulcan who made the thunderbolt. Jupiter condemned Apollo to serve for King Admetus for one year. Admetus married Alcestis with the help of Apollo but he caught a severe illness. The Fates permitted that someone could dead for him and only Alcestis was willing to. Just then Heracles came and forced the Death to resign his victim.

Phaeton: Apollo's son. He wanted to prove his identity and requested his father to let him drive the chariot for one day. But he could not control the chariot and burned the earth and the heaven. Zeus stroke him with his thunderbolt.

 

House of Cadmus

Cadmus

After Europa was taken away by Zeus, Agenor, king of Phoenicia commanded his son Cadmus to search for his sister. Cadmus consulted the oracle of Apollo and followed a cow wandering until it stopped and built the city named Thebes. His servants went to find water and was killed by a dragon. Cadmus killed the dragon and a voice commanded him to sow the (Mars' ) serpent's teeth into the earth and then there was a harvest of armed warriors. They fought each other until five survivors left and they built Thebes.

Cadmus obtained in marriage Harmonia, the daughter of Venus. Since Cadmus killed Mars' serpent, his daughters Semele and Ino, and his son Actaeon and Pentheus, all perished unhappily. Then Cadmus left Thebes and went to the country of the Enchelians. He and his wife became serpents.

King of Thebes: Cadmus → Polydorus → Labdacus → Laius → Oedipus

 

Antigone

The daughter of Oedipus. She companied his wandering till he died and then returned to Thebes.

Seven against Thebes: Her brother Eteocles and Polynices agreed to share the kingdom between them. But Eteocles refused to surrender the kingdom to his brother. Polynices fled to Adrastus, king of Argos, who gave him an army to fight his claim to the kingdom.

Tiresias: a blind prophet. He saw Minerva bathing in his youth. The goddess deprived his sight but afterwards gave him the power to see the future.

Eteocles and Polynices fought and fell by each other's hands. Creon buried Eteocles but suffered Polynices' body. Antigone buried Polynices' corpse and Creon judged her to be buried alive.

 

House of Tantalus

Tantalus

He tried to trick the gods into eating his son, he was made to stand in a pool of water beneath a fruit tree with low branches, with the fruit ever eluding his grasp, and the water always receding before he could take a drink.

Sisyphus: The founder and king of Corinth who killed visitors to show off his power. Gods punished him to roll an boulder up a hill and it rolled back down every time it neared the top.

Ixion: The son of Ares. Zeus pitied him after his terrible sin and brought him to Olympus. But Ixion grew lustful to Hera. Zeus ordered Hermes to bind Ixion to a winged fiery wheel that was always spinning.

 

Niobe

Niobe was the queen of Thebes. During the annual celebration in honor of Latona and her offspring, Apollo and Diana, Niobe persuaded the people to cancel the worship. Latona called Apollo and Diana to punish Niobe. They killed all her seven children and seven daughters. Niobe was changed to stone with her tears streaming.

Atreus and Thyestes

King of Mycenae. Atreus and his twin brother Thyestes were exiled by their father for murdering their half-brother Chrysippus in their desire for the throne of Olympia.

 

Agamemnon

Agamemnon, the general-in-chief of the Greeks, the brother of Menelaus. During his absence his wife Clytemnestra and her paramour planned to kill him at the banquet. Agamemnon's children, Electra sent Orestes secretly to his uncle Strophius, king of Phocis. Orestes grew up with the king's son Pylades. After Orestes grew up, he went back to Argos pretending to announce the death of Orestes and slew Aegisthus and Clytemnestra.

The Erinyes (avenging goddess), seized upon Orestes from land to land. Pylades accompanied him. He finally took refuge with Minerva at Athens and was acquitted by the court vote and the command of Minerva. But the Erinyes still threatened to torment all inhabitants of Athens. Athena persuaded them to break the cycle of blood to blood while promising that the goddesses will receive due honor from the Athenians and Athena. The Erinyes' name was changed into the Eumenides.

Iphigenia, the daughter of Agamemnon, was a sacrifice to Artemis in the port of Aulis when Agamemnon killed a stag which was sacred to her. When she was to be sacrificed, Artemis snatched her away and placed a hind in her place.

 

Minerva

Minerva was the goddess of wisdom. She presided over the useful and ornamental arts. She was also the goddess of defensive war.

Athens was her own city as the prize of a contest with Neptune. The tale ran that the first king of Athens, Cecrops, decided that the god who gave the most useful gift to mortals win. Neptune gave the horses; Minerva gave the olive.

Arachne, a maiden who was skillful in weaving, wanted to compare with Minerva. Minerva wove her contest with Neptune; Arachne wove the failings and errors of the gods: Leda and the swan, Danae and the golden shower, Europa and the bull. Minerva was infuriated and turned Arachne into a spider.

 

Jason and the golden fleece

In Thessaly a king and queen named Athamas and Nephele. They had two children. After a time Athamas grew indifferent to his wife. Mercury assisted Nephele and gave her a ram with a golden fleece. The ram took the children to the East. The girl Helle fell into the sea where was called the Hellespont. The boy Phryxus was hospitably received by Aeetes. Phryxus sacrificed the ram to Jupiter and gave the golden fleece to Aeetes who placed it in a consecrated grove, under the care of a sleepless dragon.

There was another kingdom in Thessaly near to that of Athamas. The king Aeson surrendered his crown to his brother Pelias on condition that he should hold it only during the minority of his son, Jason. Peliad pretended he will yield it but suggested Jason to get the Golden Fleece in the kingdom of Colchis.

Jason employed Argus to build him a vessel capable of containing fifty men named "Argo". A band of youths joined him including Hercules, Theseus, Orpheus and Nestor. They are called the Argonauts.

They crossed the Symplegades or the Clashing Islands and arrived Colchis. King Aeetes agreed to give up the golden fleece if Jason would yoke to the plough two fire-breathing bulls with brazen feet, and sow the teeth of the dragon which Cadmus had slain. Jason had previously promised his marriage with Medea, daughter of the king who was a powerful sorceress. Jason used Medea's spell to encounter safely the breath of the fire-breathing bulls and threw a stone to the armed army so that they fought each other. But Aeetes planned to attack the Argonauts. Medea told Jason and Jason took the golden fleece with Medea's aid of sleeping the dragon. They returned Thessaly, gave the golden fleece to Pelias and sacrificed the Argo to Neptune.

 

Medea and Jason

Jason wanted Medea to take some of his years and add it to his father's. Medea did it without cutting Jason's years. Pelias also wanted to grow his life. Medea pretended to agree but used a different spell. The trick was found out by Pelias. Medea ran away. Jason married Creusa, princess of Corinth. Medea sent a poisoned robe as a gift to the bride and killed her own children, and set fire to the palace, and drove her chariot to Athens where she married King Aegeus, the father of Theseus.

 

Nisus and Scylla

Minos, king of Crete, made war upon Megara. Nisus was king of Megara, and Scylla was his daughter. Megara stand as long as a certain purple lock among the hair of King Nisus remained on his head. Scylla admired Minos and cut Nisus' purple lock and sacrifice the city to him. Minos thought this was a disgrace. He and his army left Megara. Scylla jumped into the sea and seized the rudder of Minos' ship. His father Nisus became a sea-eagle striking her.

Hercules

Hercules was the son of Jupiter and Alcmena. Juno wanted to killed him when he was a babe. She sent two serpents to killed him but the babe strangled the serpents. By Juno's tricks he surrendered to Eurystheus and Eurystheus gave him several tasks called the "Twelve Labors of Hercules".

  1. Nemean lion.

  2. Hydra. This monster ravaged the country of Argos.

  3. The cleaning of the Augean stables.

  4. Obtain the girdle of the queen of the Amazons.

  5. The oxen of Geryon, a monster with three bodies.

    The Pillars of Hercules. The oxen were guarded by the giant Eurytion and his two-headed dog. Hercules killed them and brought away the oxen.

  6. The golden apples of the Hesperides. Hercules went to Mount Atlas. He persuaded Atlas to find the golden apples for him while he would carry the Heaven during Atlas' absence.

Other exploits:

  1. Victory over Antaeus, son of Gaea, who was invincible if and only if remained in contact with his mother Earth. Hercules strangled him in the air.

  2. Slew Cacus the huge giant who stole part of the cattle of Hercules.

  3. Bringing Cerberus from the lower world, and obtaining the liberty of Theseus.

  4. Hercules killed his friend Iphitus in a fit of madness and became the slave of Queen Omphale for three years. During this service he wore the dress of a woman. After this he married Dejanira. When they came to a river, the Centaur Nessus wanted to carried Dejanira away but Hercules killed him. The dying Centaur told Dejanira to take a portion of his blood it might be used to preserve the love of her husband.

  5. Hercules was fond of a maiden named Iole. Dejanira steeped the white robe which Hercules would wear in the blood of Nessus and washed out all traces of it. The magic robe hurt Hercules and he was burned on Mount Ceta. After that he went into the Heaven.

 

Achelous and Hercules

The river god Achelous told his story to Theseus. He and other suitors strove to win Dejanira. At last he and Hercules left to fight each other. Achelous changed into a snake and a bull during the fight. And Hercules rent his horn from his head. The Naiads took it, consecrated it and filled it with fragrant flowers, called it "Cornucopia".

Theseus

Theseus was the son of Aegeus, king of Athens, and of Aethra, daughter of the king of Troezen. He was brought up in Troezen. Aegeus placed his sword and shoes under a large stone and directed Aethra to sent his son to him when he grew up. Theseus took the sword and shoes. Despite of his grandfather's suggestion, he took his adventure.

  1. Periphetes, son of Vulcan, a savage who armed with a club of iron and killed the pass-by travelers.

  2. Procrustes, or the Stretcher. He had an iron bedstead to suffer the pass-by travelers.

When Theseus arrived Athens, Medea deluded Aegeus to killed this young stranger. Aegeus recognized his sword and sent Medea away to Asia.

At that time Athens was forced to sacrifice seven youths and seven maidens every year as the food of the Minotaur, a monster dwelt in Daedalus' labyrinth. Theseus offered himself as one of the victims. The ship departed under black sails, as usual, which Theseus promised his father to change for white, in case of his returning victorious. Minos' daughter Ariadne admired him and gave him a sword and a clue of thread.

Theseus killed Minotaur and left the labyrinth and took Ariadne away. One night he dreamed Minerva who warned him that Ariadne was doomed to be the wife of Bacchus. He left Ariadne at the island of Naxos.

When Theseus arrived Athens, he forgot to hang the white sails. The old king Aegeus jumped into the sea and the sea was named the Aegean Sea.

Other exploits:

  1. Expedition against the Amazons. After Hercules' attack, Theseus assailed them before their recovery and took their queen Antiope. The Amazons fought back till the city of Athens but Theseus defended.

  2. The friendship between Theseus and Pirithous. They both wanted to married Jupiter's daughter. Theseus took Helen who was still a child. Pirithous wanted to get Persephone. Pluto seized them on an enchanted rock at his palace gate.

  3. After Antiope's death, Theseus married Phaedra, daughter of Minos. Phaedra saw in Hippolytus, the son of Theseus.

 

Daedalus

Daedalus built the labyrinth for King Minos, but afterwards was shut up in a tower. He fabricated wings for himself and his young son Icarus. Daedalus warned Icarus not to fly too high for the heat will melt the wax on the wings nor too low for the damp will wet the wings. But Icarus flew too high and fell down and dead.

Daedalus grew arrogance and refused to teach his nephew Perdix. When they were together one day on the top of a high tower, Daedalus pushed him off. But Minerva changed Perdix into a bird called Partridge.

 

Orpheus and Eurydice

Orpheus was the son of Apollo and the Muse Calliope. Apollo gave him his lyre. After his marriage with Eurydice, one day Eurydice wandered with the nymphs, she was seen by the shepherd Aristaeus who chased her. Eurydice was bit by a snake during her fleeing.

Orpheus went down to Tartarus and sang to persuade Pluto to release Eurydice to the upper world. During his songs, Tantalus, Ixion, the vulture (of Prometheus), the daughters of Danaus, and Sisyphus all stopped. Pluto promised him with one condition that he should not look back to her until they have reached the upper air. When they almost arrived, Orpheus looked back and Eurydice returned to Tartarus.

Orpheus was killed by drunk maidens. Jupiter put his lyre among stars. And his soul came to Tartarus and lived with Eurydice.

 

Perseus

  1. Perseus' birth

    Perseus was the son of Jupiter and Danae. His grandfather Acrisius, alarmed by an oracle which had told him that his daughter's child would be the instrument of his death, caused the mother and child to be shut up in a chest and set adrift on the sea. A fisherman found them and took them to Polydectes, king of Seriphus.

  2. Perseus and Medusa

    When Perseus grew up, Polydectes sent him to conquer Medusa. Medusa was a beautiful maiden but wanted to challenge Minerva with her beauty. Minerva changed her into a Gorgon. Minerva lent Perseus her shield and Mercury lent him his winged shoes. Perseus used the reflection on the shield to kill Medusa.

  3. Perseus and Atlas

    After the slaughter of Medusa, Perseus went to the western limit of the earth and met Atlas. Atlas remembered an ancient prophecy that a son of Jove would rob him of his golden apples so he refused to receive Perseus. Perseus used the head of Medusa and turned Atlas into stone.

  4. Perseus and the sea-monster

    Perseus arrived at the country of the Aethiopians of which Cepheus was king. Cassiopeia the queen, had dared to compare herself to the Sea-Nymphs. They sent a sea-monster to ravage the coast. Cepheus was directed by the oracle to sacrifice his daughter Andromeda to the monster. Perseus killed the sea-monster and married Andromeda.

  5. Perseus' wedding feast

    During Perseus' wedding, Phineus, the betrothed of the virgin, came with his party demanding the maiden as his own. He and his men fought with Perseus' men. Perseus took out the head of Medusa and turned his enemies into stone.

     

Pegasus and Chimera

When Perseus cut off Medusa's head, the blood sinking into the earth produced the winged horse Pegasus. Minerva caught and tamed him and presented him to the Muses. The fountain Hippocrene, on the Muses' mountain Helicon, was opened by a kick from his hoof.

The Chimera was a fearful monster, breathing fire. The fore part of its body was a compound of the lion and the goat, and the hind part a dragon's. King Iobates sought for some hero to destroy it. A young warrior named Bellerophon came with the letters from Proetus. Iobates sent Bellerophon to kill Chimera. Under the suggestion of the prophet, Bellerophon slept in Minerva's temple and Minerva gave him a golden bridle in the dream. Bellerophon took control of Pegasus and beat Chimera with the help of Pegasus.

But Bellerophon grew arrogance and wanted to ride the horse into the Heaven. Zeus sent a gadfly to bite Pegasus and made him throw his rider. Bellerophon fell down and became lame and blind and died miserably.

Aurora and Tithonus

Aurora fell in love with Tithonus, son of Laomedon, king of Troy. She prevailed on Jupiter to grant him immortality but forgot to join his youth in the gift. Finally Tithonus became a grasshopper.

Memnon was their son, king of Aethiopians. During the Trojan war, he killed Antilochus, but then Achilles appeared and killed him.

Aurora commanded her brothers, the Winds, to convey his body to the banks of the river Esepus. In the night Aurora came with the Hours and the Pleiads. The Night spread the heaven with clouds. Aethiopians built a tomb for him. Jupiter caused the sparks and cinders of his funeral pile to be turned into birds, which, dividing into two flocks, fought over the pile till they fell into the flames. Every year at the anniversary of his death they return and celebrate his obsequies in like manner.
